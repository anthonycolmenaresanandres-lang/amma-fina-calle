# Cinematic Studio

## Purpose

Create controlled cinematic AI image/video prompts that preserve identity, depth, lighting, motion, and brand integrity.

## 9:16 Vertical Filmmaking Doctrine

Mobile-first vertical output is the default for campaign video. Because 9:16 has limited horizontal space, action should move toward or away from the lens whenever possible.

Use vertical depth:

- Foreground obstruction.
- Midground subject.
- Background atmosphere.
- Motion toward lens or away from lens.
- Closeups, reaction shots, silhouettes, and inserts.

Avoid wide horizontal action that needs a landscape frame to read.

## Z-Axis Blocking Rule

Every cinematic prompt must define foreground, midground, and background.

```txt
Z-AXIS PLACEMENT:
Foreground: [closest object or obstruction]
Midground: [main subject/action]
Background: [environment/depth cue]
```

## One Motion Rule

Each shot should contain only one primary motion: either the camera moves or the subject moves. Avoid moving both unless the shot is low-risk.

Low-risk examples:

- Static subject, slow camera push-in.
- Static camera, subject slowly turns head.
- Static camera, rain/fog moves as atmosphere only.

## Light Source As Director Rule

Lighting direction must be specified in every cinematic prompt.

Define:

- Direction: top right, behind subject, low left, overhead, etc.
- Quality: hard, soft, strobe, diffused, rim, practical.
- Color: warm, cold, blue, gold, green, firelight, lightning.
- Shadow behavior: one side dark, eyes shadowed, backlit silhouette.

## Environmental Masking Strategy

Fog, rain, darkness, lightning, silhouettes, shallow depth of field, and fast cuts are production tools for hiding AI imperfections.

Use masking when:

- Monsters are not stable.
- Hands are likely to deform.
- Background continuity is fragile.
- Text/logos would be unsafe inside generation.
- Complex crowds or combat would break.

## Monster Reveal In Pieces Rule

Do not reveal a complex monster all at once unless required. Reveal pieces:

1. Shadow crosses face.
2. Eye opens.
3. Claw/teeth flash.
4. Silhouette lifts victim.
5. Reaction closeup sells the event.

The audience understands more from reaction and fragments than from a broken full-body monster render.

## 3-5 Second Clip Discipline

Use short 3-5 second clips. Each clip should test one idea:

- One identity lock.
- One action.
- One camera rule.
- One lighting direction.
- One output format.

## AI Blocking Matrix Format

| Shot ID | Identity Lock | Environment Lock | Z-Axis Placement | Primary Motion | Camera | Lighting Source | Atmosphere | Format | Rejection Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| S01 | [lock] | [environment] | F/M/B | [one motion] | [static/push/tilt] | [direction] | [fog/rain/etc.] | 9:16 | [risk] |

## Shot Rejection Criteria

Reject if:

- Identity lock drifts.
- Camera and subject both move in a risky shot.
- Lighting direction is missing.
- Shot ignores 9:16 readability.
- Text, logo, QR, or menu words are generated by AI.
- Skin becomes plastic.
- Hands deform in a hero shot.
- Monster is fully visible but broken.
- Environment changes without approval.

## Required Outputs

- Storyboard.
- Shot matrix.
- Prompt packet.
- Micro-motion test when identity is involved.
- QA result before edit assembly.
